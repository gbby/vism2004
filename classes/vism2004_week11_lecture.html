The Net is, by design, an interruption system, a machine geared for dividing attention.
We willingly accept the loss of concentration and focus, the division of our attention and the fragmentation of our thoughts, in return for the wealth of compelling or at least diverting information we receive.

- Nicholas Carr
If you’re using a free online service, the adage goes, you are the product... attention, thus conceived, is an inert and finite resource, like oil or gold... there has even been talk of the world reaching ‘peak attention.’

Attention Economy

Attention reintroduces a principle of scarcity to the information economy.


Attention is not a commodity, but a kind of capital.


By consuming attention and making it scarce, the wealth of information creates poverty that in its turn produces the conditions for a new market to emerge.

Attention Economy (cont.)

Attention is not just scarce, but also degraded.


Attention triggers potential processes of imitation by means of which network culture produces and reproduces itself.


What is at stake is... a new translation in economic terms of the theme of imitative, swarming and contagious behaviours as characterizing networked communication.

The next time you hear someone talking about algorithms, replace the term with “God” and ask yourself if the meaning changes. Our supposedly algorithmic culture is not a material phenomenon so much as a devotional one, a supplication made to the computers people have allowed to replace gods in their minds, even as they simultaneously claim that science has made us impervious to religion.

- Ian Bogost

Questions

Do we have control of our attention? Are we losing control?


In what cases might new tools actually enable us to regain some control?
